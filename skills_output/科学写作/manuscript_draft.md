# Does Labelling Medical Advice as AI-Generated Reduce Public Trust? Evidence from Two Randomised Experiments

## Abstract

Artificial intelligence (AI) is increasingly deployed to generate health-related advice, yet little is known about how disclosing AI involvement shapes public perceptions of that advice. We conducted two pre-registered online randomised experiments (Study 1: *n* = 1,050; Study 2: *n* = 1,230, a representative sample of UK adults) in which participants viewed identical medical advice attributed to one of three sources: a human physician, an AI chatbot, or a human physician assisted by an AI chatbot. In Study 1, advice labelled as coming from a human physician was rated significantly higher on perceived empathy and reliability than advice labelled as AI-generated or human–AI collaborative (Cohen's *d* ≥ 0.26, all *P* ≤ 0.001), while perceived comprehensibility did not differ across conditions. Study 2 replicated these findings in a representative sample and further demonstrated that participants who believed AI was involved in generating the advice reported significantly lower willingness to follow it (Cohen's *d* ≥ 0.31, all *P* ≤ 0.001). These results indicate that the mere attribution of medical advice to AI—even when the content is identical—reduces public trust and willingness to act on that advice, highlighting a critical challenge for the integration of AI into patient-facing healthcare communication.

## Introduction

Large language models and other AI systems are increasingly capable of generating medical advice that rivals or matches the quality of advice produced by human clinicians[需要补充引用]. In response, healthcare organisations and technology companies are exploring the deployment of AI chatbots for patient triage, symptom checking, and health education. A key policy question is whether, and how, the disclosure of AI involvement in generating medical advice affects public perceptions of that advice.

Prior research has examined attitudes toward AI in healthcare, but the evidence base on whether labelling effects influence trust, empathy judgments, and behavioural intentions remains limited and mixed[需要补充引用]. Understanding these effects is essential because regulatory frameworks increasingly mandate transparency about AI use, yet disclosure could inadvertently undermine confidence in accurate, beneficial medical advice. Conversely, failing to disclose AI involvement raises ethical concerns about deception and informed consent.

The present research addresses this gap through two randomised experiments that isolate the effect of source labelling on perceptions of identical medical advice. We tested three hypotheses: first, that medical advice attributed to a human physician would be perceived as more reliable and empathetic than identical advice attributed to an AI chatbot; second, that a hybrid human–AI label would not fully mitigate any negative labelling effect; and third, that perceived comprehensibility would be unaffected by source labelling, as it reflects the content of the advice itself rather than its perceived source. Study 2 extended this investigation to a representative sample of UK adults and added a measure of willingness to follow the advice, enabling an assessment of whether labelling effects extend from perceptual judgments to behavioural intentions.

## Results and Discussion

### Study 1

Consistent with our hypotheses, source labelling significantly affected participants' perceptions of the medical advice on empathy and reliability, but not on comprehensibility (Table 1).

**Perceived empathy.** Advice labelled as coming from a human physician was rated significantly higher on empathy than advice labelled as coming from an AI chatbot (*t*(698) = 3.58, *P* < 0.001, Cohen's *d* = 0.27, 95% CI [0.12, 0.42]). The human physician label also elicited significantly higher empathy ratings than the human + AI label (*t*(698) = 3.44, *P* = 0.001, Cohen's *d* = 0.26, 95% CI [0.11, 0.41]). There was no significant difference in empathy ratings between the AI chatbot and human + AI conditions.

**Perceived reliability.** The same pattern emerged for reliability ratings. The human physician label yielded significantly higher reliability ratings than the AI chatbot label (*t*(698) = 3.72, *P* < 0.001, Cohen's *d* = 0.28, 95% CI [0.13, 0.43]) and the human + AI label (*t*(698) = 3.90, *P* < 0.001, Cohen's *d* = 0.29, 95% CI [0.15, 0.44]). The AI chatbot and human + AI conditions did not differ significantly from each other.

**Perceived comprehensibility.** In contrast to empathy and reliability, comprehensibility ratings did not differ significantly across the three conditions (*F* < 1), indicating that participants judged the clarity of the advice based on its content rather than its purported source.

These results demonstrate a clear labelling effect: the same medical advice was judged as less empathetic and less reliable when participants believed it had been generated by AI, either alone or in collaboration with a physician. The effect sizes were small to moderate in magnitude (Cohen's *d* ranging from 0.26 to 0.29) but were highly consistent across the two affected dimensions. Notably, the human + AI label did not confer any advantage over the AI-only label, suggesting that disclosing AI involvement—even when a human physician is also credited—may be sufficient to reduce perceived empathy and reliability to levels indistinguishable from AI-only attribution.

### Study 2

Study 2 replicated the core findings of Study 1 in a representative UK sample and extended them to behavioural intention.

**Replication of Study 1 findings.** As in Study 1, the human physician label produced significantly higher empathy and reliability ratings than both the AI chatbot and human + AI labels (*t*s ≥ 3.01, *P*s ≤ 0.003, Cohen's *d*s ≥ 0.21). Comprehensibility ratings again did not differ significantly across conditions (*t*s < 1), confirming that the labelling effect is specific to evaluative dimensions (empathy and reliability) and does not extend to content-based judgments of clarity.

**Willingness to follow advice.** Critically, when participants believed that AI was involved in generating the medical advice, their willingness to follow the advice was significantly lower. This effect was observed both when comparing the human physician label to the AI chatbot label and when comparing the human physician label to the human + AI label (*t*s ≥ 4.46, *P*s ≤ 0.001, Cohen's *d*s ≥ 0.31). The magnitude of these effects on behavioural intention was numerically larger than those observed for empathy and reliability, suggesting that the downstream consequences of AI labelling may be practically meaningful.

**Behavioural measure.** The proportion of participants who saved a link to a virtual platform containing further health information did not differ significantly across conditions (Human: 19.3%; AI: 18.5%; Human + AI: 22.9%). This null finding suggests that while explicit willingness-to-follow ratings were sensitive to source labelling, a low-stakes behavioural proxy measure was not. Several interpretations are possible: the act of saving a link may reflect curiosity or general health information-seeking rather than trust specifically in the advice; alternatively, the effect of labelling on explicit judgments may not translate to this particular behavioural outcome at the observed sample size.

### General Discussion

Across two experiments with a combined sample of 2,280 participants, we found consistent evidence that labelling medical advice as AI-generated—whether alone or in conjunction with a human physician—reduces perceptions of its empathy and reliability, and lowers willingness to follow it. These effects emerged despite all participants viewing identical advice content, underscoring that it is the label itself, rather than any difference in quality, that drives the observed disparities.

The finding that the human + AI label performed similarly to the AI-only label, and significantly worse than the human-only label, is particularly noteworthy. It suggests that merely disclosing AI involvement may trigger a negative heuristic about the advice, one that is not compensated for by the simultaneous presence of a human physician. This has direct implications for transparency policies: while disclosure of AI use is ethically important, it may carry unintended consequences for patient trust and adherence that warrant careful consideration.

The dissociation between comprehensibility and the evaluative dimensions (empathy and reliability) is theoretically informative. It indicates that participants did not simply rate AI-labelled advice more negatively on all dimensions; rather, they differentiated between the inherent clarity of the content and their trust in its source. This pattern is consistent with the elaboration likelihood model and related dual-process frameworks, in which source cues influence attitudes through peripheral-route processing when content is held constant.

This study has several limitations. First, the experiments relied on hypothetical scenarios rather than real clinical interactions; the extent to which these labelling effects persist in authentic healthcare settings, where patients have established relationships with providers and more at stake, remains unknown. Second, the effect sizes were small to moderate, and their practical significance at the population level requires further investigation. Third, the behavioural measure in Study 2 (link saving) may lack the sensitivity to detect labelling effects that are evident in self-report measures. Future research should examine whether these labelling effects are moderated by the severity of the medical condition, the objective accuracy of the advice, individual differences in AI literacy or attitudes, and repeated exposure to AI-generated health information. It will also be important to investigate whether specific framing strategies—such as emphasising the human oversight of AI systems or the evidence base underlying AI-generated recommendations—can attenuate the negative labelling effect without compromising transparency.

In conclusion, the present findings indicate that public perceptions of medical advice are shaped not only by the content of that advice but also by beliefs about its source. As AI systems become more integrated into healthcare communication, the challenge will be to design disclosure practices that maintain trust while honouring the ethical obligation of transparency.
